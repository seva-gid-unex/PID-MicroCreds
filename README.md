# Final Report: Integration of Digital Microcredentials into University Assessment

This Innovation in Teaching Project explored the integration of digital microcredentials into the assessment process of undergraduate courses at the University of Extremadura. The main objective was to evaluate whether recognising specific competencies through digital badges could enhance students' academic performance, motivation, and engagement while providing them with verifiable evidence of their achievements.

The project was implemented across several degree programmes, including Computer Engineering, Telecommunications Engineering, and Podiatry, involving courses taught at both the School of Engineering (Cáceres) and the University Centre of Plasencia. Digital microcredentials were designed and issued using the Badgecraft platform, with each credential recognising the achievement of a specific learning outcome or competency within a course. Examples include competencies in OpenMP parallel programming, VHDL, KNX home automation, scientific writing, algorithm design, and real time systems.

To assess the impact of the initiative, both quantitative and qualitative analyses were conducted. The quantitative study compared students' academic results with those obtained in previous academic years, before the introduction of microcredentials. Overall, most courses showed an improvement in average grades, while the only course that experienced a slight decrease in the average grade also recorded an increase in the number of students achieving the highest marks, which was the criterion established for earning one of its microcredentials.

The qualitative evaluation was based on a student survey. The results indicate a positive perception of the initiative, with most students reporting that microcredentials increased their motivation, encouraged greater effort, particularly in practical activities, and helped them recognise their learning achievements. Furthermore, 82.6% of respondents stated that they would like similar microcredentials to be implemented in more university courses.

Overall, the project demonstrates that digital microcredentials can be successfully integrated into the assessment of official university courses. Beyond recognising specific competencies, they contribute to improving student engagement and provide an innovative complement to competency-based assessment. The positive results obtained support the extension of this approach to additional subjects and degree programmes and encourage further research into its long-term educational impact.
